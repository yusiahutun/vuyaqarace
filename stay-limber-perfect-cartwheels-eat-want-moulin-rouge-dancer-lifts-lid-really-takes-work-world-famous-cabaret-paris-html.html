Moulin Rouge dancer reveals what it takes to work at the world-famous cabaret in Paris

A Moulin Rouge dancer has told what it is really like to work at the French cabaret institution - covering everything from being allowed to eat 'whatever you want' to the importance of warming up the hamstrings.

Isabelle Van den Bergh Cook, whose sister Claudine is also a Moulin Rouge dancer, said she was 'surprised' by how nice the atmosphere was backstage at the venue and said she does not have to worry about diet because they like dancers to have a 'womanly figure'. 

The dancer, who is from Ireland and now lives in Paris, shared an insight into an average day for a Mode documentary.

Cabaret: Isabelle Van den Bergh Cook, pictured, is a dancer at Moulin Rouge in Paris, France

Star power: Isabelle, pictured, followed in the footsteps of her sister Claudine, who is also a dancer at Moulin Rouge

Elaborate: The dancer, who is from Ireland and lives in Paris, started dancing aged three with the encouragement of her mother who is a ballet teacher

Isabelle said she started dancing aged three when her mother, who is a ballet teacher, 'popped me into class'.

Before she auditioned Moulin Rouge, which first opened in 1889, she got tips from her sister, who was already a dancer there, who advised her to work on her cartwheels.

In the video she walks around the streets of Paris during the day and shows viewers her favorite chocolate shop and cafe. 

In the store she shows off her language skills - admitting 'my French isn't perfect at all' - and eats her favorite dessert, brioche perdue. 

She says when she first moved to Paris, the cabaret venue helped her with accommodation and she lived in Montmartre.

At hoem: Isabelle, pictured with her sister Claudine in the wings, said there is a 'lovely atmosphere' backstage

Excited: The documentary shows Isabelle on stage an during costume changes, pictured

Talented: Isabelle got through competitive auditions to be selected by Moulin Rouge

She adds: 'I remember being overwhelmed by the beauty of the streets, all the little cafes...At the start I was really amazed by all this French culture, it was really something I loved.' 

Before eating the dessert, she says: 'My guilty pleasure that I'm absolutely obsessed with is called the brioche perdue and they put caramel slash toffee sauce on it. 

'Really you can kind of eat whatever you want in this job because they like you to have a womanly figure.'

At 7.30pm she arrives outside Moulin Rouge to start getting ready for her first performance of the night and enters by the stage door. 

She said the first show is at nine and the second at 11pm and she spends an hour-and-a-half on make-up and warming up.

Indulgence: Isabelle, pictured left at a cafe with a friend, said her guilty pleasure is brioche perdue, pictured

Crucial: Isabelle, pictured warming up, said stretching ham strings is vital before performances

Happy: Isabelle, pictured walking around Paris, said she feels 'settled' in the French capital

In the dressing room, where she sits in a red dressing gown, she introduces her sister who she says having been there for nearly four years 'knows the ropes'.

'When I came here it was really handy, even actually when I auditioned, she was like Izzy, you need to work on your cartwheels before you come, in the audition they do cartwheels for can-can, left and right,' she says.

'I think the fact that I have my sister here helps me, because a lot of people struggle. I actually am settled here really well.'

In the minutes before the performance, the pair are seen warming up and stretching together backstage in the wings. 

High kick: The dancers perform their most famous dance, the can-can, in the show

Revealing: Isabelle said confidence is important when auditioning for Moulin Rouge

Challenging: Isabelle, pictured left and right, said the can-can is a 'very quick dance'

She says: 'After the make-up's done I do a warm-up - you must warm up your ham strings really really well.'

Talking through the dances, she says the can-can is a 'very quick dance', adding: 'You have a lot of kicks, jump splits, you get the feeling of the olden days and what the Moulin used to be.'

She added: 'We're all in here every night working together. I was surprised to find out that it was actually a really lovely atmosphere backstage.'

Isabelle advised aspiring Moulin Rouge dancers to 'practice your cartwheels, practice your splits, really good kicks'.

In the audition she said it is important to 'be very confident, be unique, be your own person and have your own style'.
