Mallorca JELLYFISH: Brit holiday hotspot hit by thousands of stinging creatures

One picture to emerge today shows jellyfish on the beach at Port de Soller on the Spanish Balearic island's west coast.

Residents and tourists are giving the beach a wide berth since strong currents washed the dangerous sea creatures onto the sands.

It’s a short drive from party resort Magaluf – where thousands of Brits enjoyed a wild summer.

Many are booked in to return in just a few months time.

Local media report that the presence of the jellyfish on the beach at Port de Soller – some of which are unusually large – is the result of recent storms in the area.

There is no recent record of jellyfish in Magaluf though, according to authorities at Calvia town hall.

Officials in the Balearic islands have previously acted to keep jellyfish away from tourist areas.

They introduced a number of measures after dangerous Portuguese man o' wars were spotted off the smaller island of Formentera.

The potentially fatal creature is normally found in the Atlantic Ocean, as well as the Indian and Pacific Oceans.

Their presence in the Balearic islands, which lie off the eastern coast of Spain in the Mediterranean Sea, was blamed on global warming.

While these jellyfish are not believed to be man o' wars, they do deliver a painful sting.

Majorca is hugely popular with tourists, including many Brits, during the summer months but winter tourism has increased in recent years.

Last summer locals warned partying Brits to be careful of jellyfish.

One security guard said: “All they need are a few stings and they could get in right trouble in the water.

"That drunk, that late at night – it could spell disaster.”

Another said it was dangerous going skinny dipping in the sea due to the high volume of jellyfish.

They continued: “They’re crazy. There’s loads of the creatures in the sea. They come right up to the shoreline, especially at night.

“I’ve heard about many of people being stung, one French girl had a massive rash on her arm after she touched one.”

Website Holidays Majorca warned of the dangers of getting stung by jellyfish in the sea around the island.

It said: "For the vast majority of people, a jellyfish sting is just painful and has no long term or lethal implications.

"In the Mediterranean jellyfish do not attack humans, but if you touch their tentacles, they will sting as a defence mechanism.

"Usually jellyfish stings in Majorca are not critical, unless the person is allergic to the poison or has some specific disease. Even when they are not critical, they can be really painful and sore.

"The typical symptoms are pain, burning and itching sensation, swelling and redness of the area and even bleeding."
