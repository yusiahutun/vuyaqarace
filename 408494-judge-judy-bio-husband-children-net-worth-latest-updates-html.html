Judge Judy biography

Judy was born to a Jewish family from Germany, Russia, and Ukraine. She is the daughter of Murray (father) and mother Ethel Blum. Her father was a dentist in the army, and she has a younger brother, David, a dentist by profession. Judge Judy age will be 79 years later in 2021.

Origin

Susan's paternal grandfather, Jacob Israelit Blum, the son of Shaul Israelit and Rivka, was born in Odessa, in the Russian Empire. Her paternal grandmother, Lena Mininberg, the daughter of Hersh Ber Mininberg and Aydel Duckmann, was born in Russia.

Judith's maternal grandfather was Irving Silverman, who was born in Russia. Her maternal grandmother, Mary Madiefsky, the daughter of Samuel Madiefsky and Nettie, was born in New York to parents from Russia.

Early life

In an interview published on YouTube, Susan considers herself precautious and brat as a child. She was a daddy's girl and engaged in painting, ballet, and piano lessons. The family travelled a little when she was growing up, but she spent most of her childhood in Brooklyn before college.

Education

Though an average student in class, she was excellent at understanding people. Judith studied at James Madison High School in Brooklyn. She then joined the American University School of Government, Washington, D.C, where she earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in 1963.

She progressed to New York Law School, New York City, where she earned a J.D. in 1965. She was the only woman in her class to make it that far. Blum passed the bar the same year. Judge Judy holds honorary Doctor of Law degrees from Elizabethtown College and the University at Albany, SUNY.

Career

Growing up, Judy wanted to be a lawyer so as to be a politician. Judith entered private practice after passing the bar, working as a corporate lawyer for a cosmetics firm. However, she was dissatisfied and opted to quit to focus on raising her 2 children.

In 1972, Judy became a prosecuting attorney in New York City’s family court, focusing on juvenile delinquency cases. Her forthright, no-nonsense, and outspoken approach attracted Mayor Ed Koch of New York City.

In 1982, Ed appointed Mrs. Sheindlin to judge Bronx's family court, where she handled the enormous caseload with discernment and dispatch. Her sharpness and toughness did not go unnoticed. In 1986, she became the supervising judge in Manhattans family court, until 1996.

While serving as the supervising judge, Judith's impressive approach got her featured in Los Angeles Times article and the 60 Minutes TV newsmagazine show in 1993. In 1996, Judge Judy, whose popularity had spread nationwide, published her first book, Don’t Pee on My Leg and Tell Me It’s Raining: America’s Toughest Family Court Judge Speaks Out (with Josh Getlin).

Due to her popularity, TV executives proposed a courtroom show like The People’s Court that aired from 1981 to 1993. This was when Judge Judy TV show was conceptualized, and Sheindlin was made the arbiter of small-claims cases. On September 16, 1996, the show debuted, and it instantly attracted many fans because of Judith's brusque, no-nonsense approach and quick, intelligent rulings.
